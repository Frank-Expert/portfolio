    Project Title: Seasonal Trends and Time Series Analysis of Monthly Airline Passenger Traffic (1949-1960): RStudio

    Overview

    This report analyzes the Air Passengers dataset using various statistical tests and visualizations in RStudio. The dataset provides monthly totals of international airline passengers from 1949 to 1960. The analysis includes descriptive statistics, normality tests, hypothesis testing, regression analysis, and time series analysis, with detailed plots.

    Objectives

    To analyze monthly airline passenger data from 1949 to 1960, focusing on identifying trends and seasonal patterns, examining differences between periods, and evaluating overall changes in passenger traffic over time.

    Literature Review

    Time series analysis is essential for studying seasonal patterns in passenger traffic. Techniques like decomposition and regression help identify trends and seasonality. Hypothesis testing methods, such as t-tests and ANOVA, compare different periods or groups, while correlation tests assess variable relationships. Non-parametric tests address data not meeting parametric assumptions. This analysis utilizes these methods to explore the Air Passengers dataset, revealing trends, seasonality, and data relationships.

    Hypothesis

    1. The data exhibits significant seasonal variation.
    2. There is a significant difference in passenger numbers between the periods 1949-1954 and 1955-1960.
    3. There is a correlation between time and passenger numbers.
    4. The regression model accurately captures trends.

    Gaps

    The dataset lacks additional variables such as economic indicators or flight capacities, which could provide more insights into passenger trends. The reliability analysis, which is not suitable here, was omitted. Including more variables could enhance the depth of the analysis.

    Methodology

    Descriptive statistics summarize the dataset. Normality and hypothesis tests evaluate distributions and differences. Regression analysis assesses time trends. Time series decomposition identifies trend, seasonal, and random components. Plots include histograms, boxplots, scatter plots, and time series plots with enhanced details, including lines of best fit.

    Findings
    1. Descriptive Statistics

    The analysis showed that passenger counts range from 112 to 622, with a median of 265.5 and a mean of 280.3.

    2. Normality Test

    The Shapiro-Wilk test revealed that the data is not normally distributed, with a p-value less than 0.05.

    3. Independent T-Test

    A t-test indicated a significant difference in passenger numbers between the periods 1949-1954 and 1955-1960, with a p-value less than 0.05.

    4. ANOVA

    ANOVA results showed significant variation in passenger numbers across different years, with a p-value less than 0.05.

    5. Correlation Test

    Pearson correlation analysis found a significant positive correlation between time and passenger numbers (r = 0.92, p < 0.05).

    6. Regression Analysis

    The linear regression model confirmed a significant trend in passenger numbers over time, with a p-value less than 0.05 (p < 0.05).

    7. Non-Parametric Test

    The Wilcoxon rank-sum test confirmed significant differences between the periods, suitable for non-normal data.

    8. Time Series Analysis

    Decomposition identified clear trend and seasonal components in the time series data.

    The time series plot visualizes the monthly passenger counts over time, with overlaid trend and seasonal components from the time series decomposition to highlight underlying patterns and fluctuations.

This report critically analyzes the article's claims by testing them against independently collected data, hypothesis testing, and delving deeper into the broader implications of health policy.

    Summary of the Article
    Study Objectives and Scope

    Pierce et al. (2023) investigated changes in cigarette smoking and e-cigarette vaping in association with trends among U.S. adolescents and young adults. The authors drew on three large, nationally representative datasets between 1992 and 2022 to ascertain the trends for individuals aged 17-18 and 18-24. They examined the association of cigarette smoking with the use of e-cigarettes using statistical models of regression, focusing on the years 1997 to 2020. The findings showed that their work was trying to shed some light on whether declining smoking rates are due to the e-cigarette boom that happened between the years 2017 and 2019. This study provides a critical insight into the effect of e-cigarette usage on smoking rates by making this distinction despite common beliefs that they have lived together.

    Key Findings and Conclusions

    Pierce et al. (2023) indicate that from 1997, when the prevalence of cigarette smoking stood at 29.1% for young adults aged 18-24, this dropped significantly to 5.4% in 2020. In a similar vein, past 30-day smoking rates decreased among those aged 17-18 years of age from 36.8% in 1997 to 3.0% in 2022. Surprisingly, the study also reports that increased e-cigarette use among younger age groups, especially between 2017 and 2019, did not result in higher cigarette smoking among these age cohorts. They also observed that there is a relatively low correlation between smoking and vaping rates across states, with r = 0.11; this may suggest that at least the lower smoking rates are perhaps not due to the popularity of e-cigarettes but rather other public health interventions and policy measures which have been more heavily involved in reducing cigarette consumption. The paper also notes that in states where the imposition of an e-cigarette tax of more than $1 per fluid milliliter was applied, the trends of declining rates in smoking were above the average recorded nationally, suggesting once more that such taxation may more effectively deter cigarette smoking than just relying on the adoption of the e-cigarettes.

    Methodology
    Independently Collected Data and Formulation of Hypothesis

    The project aimed to verify, through new data collection on the rate of smoking among youth, the results of Pierce et al. (2023) and then conduct a statistical hypothesis test. An ethical and standardized online survey collected data from 50 adolescents and young adults. The sample population was diverse, totaling 17–24 respondents of different demographic backgrounds and smoking behavior. One of the questions posed was whether they had smoked some cigarettes during the past 30 days, and the rates can be compared to those presented in the article. Our null hypothesis, H₀, is in correspondence with the statement of the study, which claimed that the actual proportion of teen smokers was around 3% for the 17–18 age group and around 5% for the 18–24 years of age group, as found by Pierce et al., (2023). On the other hand, the alternative hypothesis, Hₐ, is that the proper proportion of teenage smokers differs from these reported rates and may be inaccurate or vary depending on regional differences, temporal factors, or sampling methods.

    Analysis and Hypothesis Testing

    We will analyze the claim quantitatively by conducting a proportion hypothesis test for each age group.
